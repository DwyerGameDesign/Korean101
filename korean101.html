한글 · Hangul

The alphabet you can learn in an afternoon

Korean isn't memorized symbol-by-symbol like Chinese — it's a true alphabet of 14 consonants and 10 basic vowels, designed in 1443 to be easy. Letters stack into little square syllable blocks. Tap a letter to hear it in a sample syllable.

How letters stack into blocks
ㅎ + ㅏ + ㄴ→ 한han
ㄱ + ㅡ + ㄹ→ 글geul
Put them together: 한글  han-geul — the name of the alphabet itself.

There are also 5 “tense” consonants (ㄲ ㄸ ㅃ ㅆ ㅉ) and some combined vowels (ㅐ eh, ㅔ eh, ㅚ weh…), but the letters above are the backbone.

존댓말 · Speech levels

The respect dial

Korean verbs change their ending depending on who you're speaking to. With your mother-in-law you stay on the polite side of the dial — never casual. Here's one idea, “did you sleep well,” said three ways:

반말 casual
잘 잤어
Close friends, kids, your spouse. Not with her.
–요 polite · your default
잘 잤어요
Warm and respectful — your everyday register with her. The whole phrasebook lives here.
–습니다 formal
잘 잤습니다
Extra-formal. Lives in set phrases like 감사합니다 and 잘 먹겠습니다.

Calling her 엄마 is about closeness; the polite verb ending is about respect. With a mother-in-law, you keep both at once.

높임말 · Honorific verbs

Special “elevated” verbs for elders

For someone you respect, a few everyday verbs swap to a higher-respect form. You've already been using these in the phrasebook without noticing.

Plain verbRespectful form (for her)You already say…
먹다 — eat드시다 → 드세요더 드세요
자다 — sleep주무시다 → 주무세요안녕히 주무세요
있다 — be / stay계시다 → 계세요같이 계셔서…
말하다 — speak말씀하시다말씀해 주세요

The general trick is a little –(으)시– slipped inside a verb (가다 → 가시다 “to go”), which is exactly why so many polite forms end in –세요.

어순 · Word order

The verb always comes last

Korean is Subject – Object – Verb. Small tags called particles attach to words to mark their job, so word order is flexible — and in casual speech particles are often dropped.

저는jeo-neunI (topic)
김치를gim-chi-reulkimchi (object)
좋아해요jo-ah-heh-yolike ← verb last
“I like kimchi.”  tap to hear the whole sentence

Handy particles: 은/는 (topic), 이/가 (subject), 을/를 (object), 에 (to / at). Leave them out and you'll still be understood — context carries a lot.

숫자 · Numbers

Two sets of numbers (yes, two)

Korean uses native numbers for counting things, people, and age, and Sino-Korean numbers for dates, money, and minutes. Tap any to hear it.

Native — things, people, age, hours
Sino-Korean — money, dates, minutes

No need to master both right now — just know why you'll sometimes hear two completely different words for the same number.

발음 · Sound rules

Three sound-changes that trip people up

  • s becomes “sh” before an “ee” sound. 시 is shi — so 식사 is shik-ssah, not sik-sa.
  • Letters link across blocks. A final consonant slides onto the next block's vowel: 음식이 → eum-shi-gi, not eum-sik-i.
  • Tense consonants are sharper. ㄲ ㄸ ㅃ ㅆ ㅉ are tighter, harder versions — 할게요 comes out hal-kke-yo.

These are already built into the “say-it-like-this” spellings in the phrasebook — so trust those and copy the audio.
